The legend of the Mexican eagle on a cactus comes from the Aztec people's quest to find their promised land, guided by their god Huitzilopochtli, who told them to build their city where they'd see an eagle devouring a serpent on a prickly pear cactus. This divine sign appeared on an island leading to the founding of their capital, Tenochtitlán (modern-day Mexico City) around 1325, and this image became the national symbol of Mexico.

The most famous Aztec ruins in Mexico center around the former capital, Tenochtitlán, primarily seen today at the Templo Mayor in Mexico City, but also at nearby Teotihuacán, a pre-Aztec city later revered by them, featuring massive Sun and Moon Pyramids, while other significant sites include the stunning ruins of Chichén Itzá, one of the seven wonders of the world.

This image is a painting of the aztec legends, Popocatépetl and Iztaccíhuat. Both were transformed into mountains by the gods after dying of sorrow and grief, respectively, representing eternal love, like the aztec verion of Romeo and Juliet.

El Charro Negro is a legend originated in Mexican folklore in remote places. El charro appears at dusk in streets and lonely sidewalks, always looking for a pedestrian to offer gold coins. After getting tired of his misery, the man invoked the Devil, who answered and offered him immense amounts of money and riches in exchange for his soul, to which the man accepted. the man tried to escape from his debt on his horse and with a bag full of gold coins. But he was caught and was condemned to wander in the bones and dressed as a charro collecting from the Devil's debtors.

La Llorona ("The Weeping Woman") is a famous Mexican folklore figure, a ghostly woman who eternally roams near rivers and lakes, crying for her drowned children, whom she often drowned herself in fits of jealous rage or despair after being betrayed by her lover
